## 1. Executive Summary

This document synthesizes research across cognitive science, entrepreneurial psychology, AI agent architectures, and knowledge management to establish the academic and industry foundations for Project Visionary: an AI agent system that can take a raw idea and mature it into a fully realized product by thinking like a product visionary.

### Key Findings

**Most useful frameworks for Project Visionary (ranked by applicability):**

1. **Effectuation Theory** (Sarasvathy) — The single most relevant framework. Expert entrepreneurs do not predict the future; they control it using available means. This maps directly to how a founder agent should reason: start with what you have, build partnerships, embrace surprises, limit downside. The five effectuation principles provide a concrete decision-making scaffold for the Visionary Agent.

2. **Tree-of-Thought Reasoning** (Yao et al.) — The best-fit AI architecture for product ideation. Unlike Chain-of-Thought's linear reasoning, ToT explores multiple product directions simultaneously, evaluates each path, and backtracks when necessary. This maps directly to how a founder considers multiple product angles before committing.

3. **Constitutional AI for Product Values** (Anthropic) — The mechanism for encoding "taste" into the system. Instead of a safety constitution, Project Visionary needs a product constitution: a set of principles that the system uses to self-critique and refine product decisions. This is how we operationalize founder quality standards.

4. **Nonaka's SECI Model** — The knowledge management framework for converting the founder's tacit knowledge (gut feel, aesthetic preferences, quality instincts) into explicit, encodable knowledge that agents can use. The externalization phase (tacit to explicit) is the critical bottleneck and the primary deliverable of Phase 1's profiling work.

5. **Case-Based Reasoning** — The retrieval mechanism for product decisions. Past decisions (from Elderle, QCR, and other projects) become cases that the agent retrieves, adapts, and applies to new situations. This gives the system a form of "experience."

6. **Multi-Agent Debate** — The quality assurance mechanism. Instead of one agent making decisions, multiple agents with different roles (Visionary, Critic, Strategist) debate product directions, catching blind spots that any single perspective would miss.

**What does not yet exist (gaps we must close):**

- No framework for encoding aesthetic "taste" as a computable function that goes beyond simple preference matching
- No architecture for maintaining product vision coherence across months of development
- No established method for an AI agent to make the creative leaps that differentiate visionary products from competent ones
- No validated approach to simulating a specific founder's decision patterns with enough fidelity to be useful as a review checkpoint

## 2. Cognitive Framework Analysis

### 2.1 Design Thinking (Stanford d.school)

**Origin:** The Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford (d.school), building on work by IDEO's Tim Brown and David Kelley.

**The Five Stages:**

1. **Empathize** — Deeply understand the user's experience through observation, interaction, and immersion. This is not survey-based; it requires direct engagement with the user's context, environment, and emotional state.

2. **Define** — Synthesize empathy insights into a clear, actionable problem statement (a "Point of View" or "How Might We" statement). The quality of the problem definition determines the quality of all downstream ideation.

3. **Ideate** — Generate a wide range of possible solutions without premature judgment. Techniques include brainstorming, mind mapping, worst-possible-idea, and SCAMPER. The goal is quantity and diversity, not immediate feasibility.

4. **Prototype** — Build quick, cheap, tangible representations of ideas to learn from. Prototypes are not demos; they are learning tools. The motto is "fail fast, fail cheap."

5. **Test** — Put prototypes in front of real users and gather feedback. Testing often loops back to Empathize (new user insights) or Define (refined problem statement).

**Key Characteristics:**
- Non-linear and iterative — stages can run in parallel, out of order, or repeat
- Human-centered — every decision references back to user needs
- Bias toward action — prototyping is valued over planning
- Collaborative — cross-functional teams bring diverse perspectives

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Design Thinking provides the **outer loop** of the Visionary Agent system. Every product idea must pass through empathy (who is this for and why do they care?), definition (what is the actual problem?), ideation (what are the possible solutions?), prototyping (what does a minimal version look like?), and testing (does it actually work for users?).

However, Design Thinking alone is insufficient. It is a process framework, not a decision-making framework. It tells you what steps to follow but not how to make the judgment calls within each step. It does not explain why Steve Jobs chose one design over another, or why a founder pivots from one direction to another. For those deeper decision patterns, we need the frameworks below.

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The **Research Agent** operates in Empathize/Define mode
- The **Visionary Agent** operates in Ideate mode
- The **Builder Agents** operate in Prototype mode
- The **Verifier Agent** operates in Test mode
- The loop structure ensures iteration, not waterfall execution

### 2.2 Jobs-to-Be-Done Theory (Clayton Christensen)

**Origin:** Tony Ulwick created the concept in 1990; Clayton Christensen popularized it through his 2003 book *The Innovator's Solution* and his 2016 *Competing Against Luck*.

**Core Insight:** Customers do not buy products. They "hire" products to get a job done. Between 75% and 85% of all new products launched fail financially because they do not target a job that people are actually trying to get done.

**The Three Dimensions of Every Job:**

1. **Functional** — What the customer needs to accomplish practically (e.g., "I need to get to work quickly")
2. **Social** — How the customer wants to be perceived by others (e.g., "I want to look successful")
3. **Emotional** — How the customer wants to feel (e.g., "I want to feel safe and in control")

The classic example is the milkshake study: McDonald's customers were "hiring" milkshakes not for taste but for the job of "make my boring commute more interesting and keep me full until lunch." No demographic analysis revealed this; only observing the actual usage context did.

**JTBD vs Traditional Market Research:**

| Traditional Approach | JTBD Approach |
|---------------------|---------------|
| Segment by demographics | Segment by jobs |
| Ask "What features do you want?" | Ask "What are you trying to accomplish?" |
| Compete on feature parity | Compete on job completion |
| Innovation = better product | Innovation = better job fulfillment |

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

JTBD provides the **analytical lens** for the Research Agent and the Visionary Agent. When evaluating a product idea, the system should ask:

- What job is the user trying to get done?
- What are they currently "hiring" to do this job?
- What are the functional, social, and emotional dimensions?
- Where is the current solution failing (overserved? underserved? non-consumption?)

For the Elderle project, the JTBD analysis reveals: the elderly person is not "hiring" a chatbot. They are hiring a companion to do the job of "make me feel less alone and more confident about my health decisions." The caregiver is hiring a system to do the job of "give me peace of mind that my parent is safe and engaged." These are fundamentally emotional and social jobs, not functional ones.

For the QCR project, the car enthusiast is hiring a tool to do the job of "help me feel smart about my vehicle and make better decisions." The gamification layer serves the social job of "let me show others I know my stuff."

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- Every product brief generated by the Visionary Agent must include a JTBD analysis
- The Research Agent validates ideas against actual user jobs, not assumed needs
- The Critic Agent challenges ideas that target features rather than jobs
- The Founder Simulator uses JTBD alignment as a quality signal

### 2.3 First Principles Thinking

**Origin:** Aristotle defined a first principle as "the first basis from which a thing is known." Popularized in the modern context by Elon Musk, who applies it as a physics-derived methodology.

**The Method:**

1. **Identify assumptions** — List everything you believe about the problem, including things you have never questioned
2. **Break down to fundamentals** — Ask "What do I know to be absolutely true?" Strip away all convention, analogy, and best practice
3. **Rebuild from the ground up** — Construct a new solution using only the fundamental truths, unconstrained by how things have been done before

**Contrast with Analogical Reasoning:**

Most people reason by analogy: "Company X did Y, so we should do something similar." This is efficient but constraining. It assumes that the solution space has already been explored and that incremental improvement is the best strategy. First principles thinking rejects this assumption entirely.

**The SpaceX Example:**

Musk's team asked: "What is a rocket made of?" Answer: aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. Raw material cost: approximately 2% of the finished rocket price. Conclusion: the high cost of rockets is not a physics constraint but an industry convention. SpaceX cut launch costs by 10x by assembling rockets in-house.

**When to Use First Principles vs When to Use Analogy:**

First principles thinking is expensive (time, cognitive effort). It should be used when:
- The existing solutions are orders of magnitude suboptimal
- The problem space has been dominated by a few players who may have converging assumptions
- You are entering a market where breakthrough innovation (not incremental improvement) is required
- A constraint that everyone accepts might not actually be a constraint

Analogical reasoning should be used when:
- The problem is well-understood and the solution space is efficiently explored
- Speed matters more than optimality
- The risk of unconventional approaches outweighs the potential reward

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

First Principles Thinking provides the **deconstructive analysis** capability for the system. The Visionary Agent should be able to:

- Take any product category and decompose it to fundamentals
- Identify which "rules" of the category are actually conventions, not constraints
- Propose solutions that violate conventions but respect fundamentals

This is particularly important for the "creative leap" moments in product development. The QCR transformation from "Charlotte car tools website" to "gamified national automotive platform" was a first-principles move: the fundamental truth is "people want to feel smart about their vehicles," not "people want a VIN decoder."

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The Visionary Agent uses first-principles decomposition when exploring new product directions
- The Critic Agent uses it to challenge conventional assumptions in proposals
- The Strategist Agent uses it when cost/timeline estimates seem unreasonably high

### 2.4 Lateral Thinking (Edward de Bono)

**Origin:** Dr. Edward de Bono coined the term "Lateral Thinking" in 1967 in his book *New Think: The Use of Lateral Thinking*. He later published the *Six Thinking Hats* method in 1985.

**Lateral Thinking vs Vertical Thinking:**

| Vertical Thinking | Lateral Thinking |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Sequential, logical | Non-sequential, generative |
| Builds on existing patterns | Creates new patterns |
| Evaluates ideas during generation | Defers evaluation |
| Moves in established directions | Moves sideways to new directions |
| Selective (chooses) | Generative (creates options) |

The key insight: lateral thinking is generative while vertical thinking is selective, and the two styles work together. Lateral thinking produces new approaches; vertical thinking develops them. Both are necessary.

**The Six Thinking Hats:**

De Bono's Six Thinking Hats method structures group thinking by separating different thinking modes:

1. **White Hat** — Facts and information. "What do we know? What do we need to find out?"
2. **Red Hat** — Emotions and intuition. "How do I feel about this? What does my gut say?"
3. **Black Hat** — Caution and critical judgment. "What could go wrong? What are the risks?"
4. **Yellow Hat** — Optimism and benefits. "What are the advantages? Why could this work?"
5. **Green Hat** — Creativity and new ideas. "What alternatives exist? What if we tried...?"
6. **Blue Hat** — Process control. "Where are we in our thinking? What should we do next?"

**Lateral Thinking Techniques:**

- **Random Entry** — Introduce a random, unrelated concept and force connections to the problem
- **Provocation** — Make deliberately absurd statements (prefixed with "PO") to escape established patterns
- **Challenge** — Question why something is done a particular way, not to find fault but to find alternatives
- **Concept Extraction** — Extract the underlying concept from a solution and apply it in a different way
- **Analogy** — Find parallels in completely different fields

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Lateral Thinking provides the **creative divergence** capability that other frameworks lack. Design Thinking tells you to ideate; Lateral Thinking tells you *how* to ideate effectively.

The Six Thinking Hats map directly to Project Visionary's agent roles:
- **White Hat** → Research Agent
- **Red Hat** → Founder Simulator (gut reactions)
- **Black Hat** → Critic Agent
- **Yellow Hat** → Visionary Agent (opportunity identification)
- **Green Hat** → Visionary Agent (idea generation)
- **Blue Hat** → Strategist Agent (process orchestration)

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The Visionary Agent uses lateral thinking techniques (random entry, provocation, concept extraction) during ideation
- The multi-agent debate system cycles through "hats" to ensure all perspectives are covered
- The system can be prompted to apply specific hats to overcome particular thinking blocks

### 2.5 Effectuation Theory (Saras Sarasvathy)

**Origin:** Dr. Saras Sarasvathy, Darden Professor at UVA, discovered effectuation in the late 1990s through in-depth studies of how expert entrepreneurs think, act, and make decisions. Her research, conducted under Nobel Laureate Herbert Simon, found that 65% of successful entrepreneurs used effectual logic 75% of the time. Published as *Effectuation: Elements of Entrepreneurial Expertise* in 2009. Sarasvathy received the 2022 Global Award for Entrepreneurship Research.

**Effectuation vs Causation:**

| Causation (Traditional) | Effectuation (Expert Entrepreneurs) |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Start with a specific goal | Start with available means |
| Predict the future | Control the future |
| Expected return analysis | Affordable loss analysis |
| Beat the competition | Build partnerships |
| Avoid surprises | Leverage surprises |
| Follow the plan | Co-create the path |

**The Five Principles:**

1. **Bird-in-Hand** — Start with who you are, what you know, and whom you know. Do not begin with a predetermined goal or pre-envisioned opportunity. Take stock of your means beyond money. This principle rejects the "start with the end in mind" approach of causal reasoning.

2. **Affordable Loss** — Think in terms of what you are willing to lose rather than what you expect to make. Expert entrepreneurs do not make elaborate financial projections; they decide what downside they can live with and invest up to that limit. This enables faster experimentation with lower stakes.

3. **Crazy Quilt** — Focus on building partnerships rather than beating competitors. Entrepreneurs start without assuming a predetermined market exists. Instead, they talk to potential stakeholders and co-create the venture with whoever is willing to commit real resources (time, money, expertise). Each new partner changes the venture's direction.

4. **Lemonade** — Turn the unexpected into the profitable. Expert entrepreneurs do not just tolerate surprises; they actively seek to exploit them. A customer complaint becomes a feature idea. A supply chain disruption becomes a new business model. This is the "yes, and" principle.

5. **Pilot-in-the-Plane** — Emphasize control, co-creation, and shaping the future rather than trying to predict it. The future is not something that happens to you; it is something you create through your actions and commitments.

**Why Effectuation is the Most Relevant Framework:**

Effectuation describes exactly how the Elderle and QCR projects actually evolved:

- **Bird-in-Hand:** The founder started with what was available (Claude, existing infrastructure, personal knowledge of elder care / automotive) and built outward
- **Affordable Loss:** Features were developed incrementally, each small enough to be discarded if they did not work
- **Crazy Quilt:** The AI assistant itself became a "partner" that co-created the product direction through research and suggestions
- **Lemonade:** Many of the best features (gamification in QCR, synthetic users in Elderle) emerged from unexpected tangential ideas
- **Pilot-in-the-Plane:** The founder shaped the product direction actively rather than following market research

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**

Effectuation should be the **default reasoning mode** of the Visionary Agent:

- When generating new product ideas, start from available means (existing codebase, team skills, user base)
- When evaluating proposals, use affordable loss (what is the downside if this fails?) rather than expected return (what is the upside if this succeeds?)
- When expanding scope, use crazy quilt thinking (who else could contribute to this? what partnerships would change the direction?)
- When encountering surprises (user feedback, technical limitations, competitive moves), use lemonade thinking (how can this surprise become an advantage?)
- The overall system should operate as a pilot-in-the-plane, actively shaping product direction rather than trying to predict market outcomes

### 2.6 Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne)

**Origin:** W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne, professors at INSEAD, published *Blue Ocean Strategy* in 2005.

**Core Concept:** Instead of competing in existing market spaces (red oceans, bloody with competition), create uncontested market spaces (blue oceans) where competition is irrelevant. This requires the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation AND low cost — what Kim and Mauborgne call "value innovation."

**Key Analytical Tools:**

1. **Strategy Canvas** — A graph plotting the key competitive factors in an industry and how existing players score on each. This visualizes where competition is concentrated and where blue ocean opportunities exist.

2. **Four Actions Framework:**
   - **Eliminate** — Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?
   - **Reduce** — Which factors should be reduced well below the industry's standard?
   - **Raise** — Which factors should be raised well above the industry's standard?
   - **Create** — Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

3. **Six Paths Framework** — Look across alternative industries, strategic groups, buyer groups, complementary products, functional-emotional appeal, and time trends.

**The Cirque du Soleil Example:**

Cirque eliminated animal shows, star performers, and multiple rings. Reduced fun/humor and thrill/danger. Raised the venue environment to a unique, refined experience. Created a theme, artistic music/dance, and a refined watching environment. Result: a blue ocean that made the competition irrelevant.

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Blue Ocean Strategy provides the **market positioning** framework:
- The Strategy Canvas can be used to map any product idea against existing competition
- The Four Actions Framework provides a structured way to differentiate
- The Six Paths Framework provides systematic methods for finding new market spaces

The Elderle project is a blue ocean play: it does not compete with existing chatbots or medical apps. It creates an uncontested space at the intersection of companionship, clinical safety, and caregiver support. The QCR project similarly creates a blue ocean by combining automotive tools, gamification, and community — a combination that no existing competitor offers.

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The Research Agent uses the Strategy Canvas to map competitive landscapes
- The Visionary Agent uses the Four Actions Framework when refining product ideas
- The Critic Agent validates that proposals achieve genuine differentiation, not incremental improvement

### 2.7 Amazon Working Backwards (Jeff Bezos)

**Origin:** Jeff Bezos and the Amazon leadership team developed the "Working Backwards" methodology, centered on the PR/FAQ (Press Release / Frequently Asked Questions) document.

**The Process:**

1. **Write the press release first** — Before any code, design, or business plan, write the press release that would announce the finished product to the public. This forces you to articulate: Who is the customer? What is the problem? How does this solve it? Why should anyone care?

2. **Write the FAQ** — Answer the hard questions: How will this make money? What are the technical challenges? Why haven't competitors done this? What will customers dislike?

3. **Iterate on the document** — The PR/FAQ goes through multiple rounds of review and revision. Jeff Bezos and the AWS team spent over a year writing, revising, debating, and discussing PR/FAQs before building AWS.

4. **Work backwards from the finished vision** — Once the press release is compelling and the FAQs are answered, the team works backwards to figure out how to build it.

**Key Insight:** Writing a press release is a forcing function. It ensures that the creator of the idea is focused on the customer, not on the technology or the business model. If the press release is boring, the product will be boring.

**Bezos on the Cost:** "The working backward process is a huge amount of work. But it will save you even more work later."

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Working Backwards provides the **output format** for the Visionary Agent's proposals. Every product idea should be expressed as a PR/FAQ before any implementation begins. This:

- Forces clarity of thought about who the customer is and why they care
- Creates a testable artifact (show the PR to potential users — would they be excited?)
- Establishes success criteria before development starts
- Makes the vision concrete enough for all agents in the pipeline to align on

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The Visionary Agent produces PR/FAQ documents as its primary output format
- The Founder Simulator evaluates PR/FAQs for "would the founder be excited by this press release?"
- The Strategist Agent works backwards from the PR/FAQ to create implementation plans
- PR/FAQs become the contract between the vision phase and the build phase

### 2.8 Paul Graham / Y Combinator Approach

**Origin:** Paul Graham, co-founder of Y Combinator, published 188 essays from 1993 to 2020 (approximately 500,000 words) on startups, programming, and product thinking.

**Key Product Thinking Principles:**

1. **Do not try to think of startup ideas.** The best startup ideas emerge organically from problems the founder personally experiences. Forced brainstorming produces derivative ideas; living at the frontier of a domain produces genuine insights.

2. **Make something people want.** This is YC's motto. It sounds obvious, but most failed startups build things people do not want. The test: are people pulling the product from you, or are you pushing it onto them?

3. **It is better to make a few users love you than a lot ambivalent.** Depth of engagement matters more than breadth. A product that 100 people love is more valuable than a product that 10,000 people find "okay."

4. **Do things that do not scale.** In the early stages, do manual, unscalable things to understand users deeply. Automate later.

5. **Launch fast and iterate.** The idea will evolve, and that evolution is where the real product emerges. Delaying launch to "get it right" is almost always wrong.

6. **Let your idea evolve.** The startup idea at launch is rarely the startup idea that succeeds. Founders must be flexible enough to follow where user feedback leads.

**Founder Qualities (per YC evaluation):**
- Determination
- Flexibility
- Imagination
- Naughtiness (willingness to break rules)
- Friendship (among co-founders)

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Graham's approach provides the **iteration philosophy** for the system. Product development is not a linear process from idea to launch; it is a evolutionary process where the idea mutates through contact with reality.

This directly describes how Elderle evolved: the initial concept (companion AI for elderly) mutated through contact with research (therapeutic stages), user needs (crisis detection), technical exploration (knowledge graphs), and founder vision (synthetic user simulation). Each mutation made the product stronger.

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The system should not over-plan before building; launch fast, iterate based on feedback
- The Visionary Agent should evaluate ideas on "do a few people love this?" not "will a lot of people use this?"
- The system should be structured to allow organic idea evolution, not force pre-planned execution
- Anti-pattern: spending months on research before building anything

### 2.9 Stripe, Notion, Linear Product Cultures

**Stripe:**
- Deep-thinking written culture focused on getting to the right answer
- Runs on long-form written documents (not meetings)
- Someone goes deep on a topic, then distills it for everyone else
- "Build multi-decade abstractions" — think 10-30 years out, not 3-5 years

**Linear:**

- Asynchronous, structured review processes

These principles should be embedded in how the agent system operates. The system should produce written artifacts, think in long time horizons, enforce quality gates, consider system-wide implications of every change, and use structured review processes.

## 3. Psychological Profiling Methodology

### 3.1 Big Five Personality Traits and Entrepreneurial Decisions

**The Big Five Model (OCEAN):**

1. **Openness to Experience** — Imagination, curiosity, willingness to explore unconventional ideas
2. **Conscientiousness** — Organization, reliability, goal-orientation, systematic execution
3. **Extraversion** — Energy from social interaction, networking, assertiveness
4. **Agreeableness** — Cooperation, trust-building, conflict avoidance
5. **Neuroticism** — Emotional reactivity, stress sensitivity, anxiety

**Research Findings on Entrepreneurial Success:**

Four of the Big Five traits are consistently linked to both entrepreneurial intention and actual performance:
- **High Openness** — Drives innovation and opportunity recognition. Entrepreneurs high in openness are more likely to pursue novel product directions.
- **High Conscientiousness** — Drives execution and long-term profitability. Critical for actually shipping products, not just ideating.
- **High Extraversion** — Drives networking and partnership building. Important for the Crazy Quilt principle of effectuation.
- **Low Neuroticism (High Emotional Stability)** — Critical for perseverance through setbacks and maintaining decision quality under stress.

**Agreeableness is Context-Dependent:**
- High agreeableness fosters collaboration and trust
- But in highly dynamic markets, overly agreeable entrepreneurs struggle with decisive risk-taking and competitive positioning
- The best founders modulate agreeableness based on context: agreeable with users and partners, assertive with competitors and mediocre work

**How This Applies to Founder Profiling:**

The Big Five provides a quantitative baseline for the Founder Decision Profile. By analyzing the founder's:
- Commit messages and document revisions (linguistic markers of personality traits)
- Decision patterns (which risks were taken vs avoided)
- Feedback patterns (what triggers pushback vs approval)
- Collaboration patterns (how work is delegated and reviewed)

We can estimate the founder's Big Five profile and use it to calibrate the Founder Simulator agent.

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**
- The Founder Simulator's decision thresholds are calibrated by the founder's Big Five profile
- High Openness → more accepting of unconventional ideas
- High Conscientiousness → stricter quality gates
- Modulated Agreeableness → collaborative with good ideas, sharp with mediocre ones
- Low Neuroticism → steady decision quality under uncertainty

### 3.2 Cognitive Style Analysis: Intuitive vs Analytical

**Two Dimensions of Cognitive Style:**

**Dimension 1: Intuitive vs Analytical**
- **Intuitive** — Relies on pattern recognition, gut feel, and holistic assessment. Fast but sometimes biased.
- **Analytical** — Relies on systematic decomposition, data analysis, and logical reasoning. Thorough but sometimes slow.

**Dimension 2: Convergent vs Divergent**
- **Convergent** — Identifies the single best solution from multiple options. Good at evaluation, accuracy, and deduction.
- **Divergent** — Generates multiple possible solutions. Good at fluency (many ideas), originality (unique ideas), flexibility (diverse ideas), and elaboration (detailed ideas).

**Key Research Findings:**

Recent research (Frontiers, 2025) shows that actual creative production requires both convergent and divergent thinking — the joint contribution is critical to creativity. Those who generate more novel, unique ideas are also better at identifying single correct solutions from alternatives.

Furthermore, the experiential (intuitive) thinking style maps onto Kahneman's System 1, while the rational (analytical) thinking style maps onto System 2. Neither is superior; both are necessary, and the ability to switch between them contextually is the hallmark of expert decision-making.

Convergent thinking relies on strong top-down cognitive control (focused attention, excluding distractions), while divergent thinking benefits from low-intensity control (flexible, rapid shifting between viewpoints).

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

The founder's cognitive style profile determines when the system should present ideas intuitively (visual mockups, gut-check questions) vs analytically (data, comparisons, logical arguments). It also determines:

- **Ideation mode:** Should the Visionary Agent generate many diverse options (divergent) or narrow down to the best option quickly (convergent)?
- **Evaluation mode:** Should the Critic Agent use systematic checklists (analytical-convergent) or holistic quality judgments (intuitive-convergent)?
- **Presentation mode:** Should proposals be presented as data-driven arguments or as narrative stories?

### 3.3 Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 Applied to Product Decisions

**Core Framework:**

- **System 1** — Fast, automatic, intuitive. Operates with little conscious effort. Responsible for snap judgments, pattern recognition, and emotional responses.
- **System 2** — Slow, deliberate, analytical. Requires conscious effort. Responsible for complex reasoning, calculation, and careful evaluation.

Both systems work in tandem. System 1 generates initial impressions; System 2 can override them with careful analysis (or lazily rubber-stamp them).

**Application to Product Decisions:**

| Decision Type | System | Example |
|--------------|--------|---------|
| "This design feels wrong" | System 1 | Founder rejects a UI mockup within 2 seconds |
| "Is this technically feasible?" | System 2 | Team analyzes architecture options over days |
| "Users will love this" | System 1 | Founder predicts user delight from a feature concept |
| "Will this scale to 10x users?" | System 2 | Performance analysis and capacity planning |
| "The name should be Quinn" | System 1 | Creative naming decision from pattern/emotion |
| "Should we use PostgreSQL or MongoDB?" | System 2 | Technical evaluation against requirements |

**When Founders Use Each System:**

Research on expert decision-making shows that experienced founders develop trained intuition — their System 1 becomes reliable for decisions within their domain of expertise. This is what Malcolm Gladwell described as "thin-slicing" in *Blink*: experts can make accurate judgments from minimal information because their pattern recognition has been trained by years of experience.

However, founders also know when NOT to trust their intuition:
- In unfamiliar domains (new market, new technology)
- When the stakes are very high (bet-the-company decisions)
- When their emotional state might bias them (frustration, excitement, fatigue)

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

The Founder Simulator must model both systems:

- **System 1 responses:** "The founder would look at this mockup and feel..." — Fast aesthetic and emotional judgments based on the founder's taste profile
- **System 2 responses:** "The founder would want to see data on..." — Analytical evaluation requests based on the founder's decision patterns

The system should also know when the founder switches between systems:
- Visual design → System 1 (taste, gut feel)
- Architecture decisions → System 2 (analysis, trade-offs)
- Feature scope → System 1 first (is this exciting?), then System 2 (is this feasible?)
- Quality evaluation → System 1 (does this feel right?) confirmed by System 2 (does it pass the gates?)

### 3.4 The Psychology of "Taste" in Product Design

**What is Taste?**

Taste is not mystical intuition. Research psychologists at UC Berkeley (Steve Palmer and Art Shimamura) found that taste and aesthetic preferences are directly related to knowledge. Preferences are stored as symbolic correlations between objects, colors, shapes, and proportions based on life experiences. Taste is pattern recognition trained by exposure, refined by critique, and made consistent through practice.

More precisely, taste is "the ability to respond consistently and appropriately to aesthetic stimuli in ways that correlate with expert or community standards." It is a measurable capacity, not an ineffable gift.

**Steve Jobs' Approach:**

Jobs' aesthetic process was intuitive but trained. He:
- Positioned himself at the intersection of art and technology from early in his career
- Studied calligraphy and font design, developing visual literacy that most technologists lack
- Rejected focus groups, instead trusting his own trained judgment and the judgment of his design team
- Would "point to models and sketches he liked, and dump on the ones he didn't" — a binary taste signal that Jony Ive used to refine designs
- Wanted Apple to lead and be the industry tastemaker, not follow user requests

**The "Taste as Judgment" Framework:**

Judgment allows a leader to sense when a strategy is sound but mistimed, when a hire looks impressive but feels wrong. It is the ability to perceive salience — to notice what matters amid noise. This goes beyond aesthetic preferences to strategic and operational decisions.

**"Taste is the New Bottleneck" (2026):**

A highly relevant 2026 article argues that as AI agents become better at execution, taste becomes the strategic bottleneck. The key claims:

- Models cannot "learn taste" in the human sense, but they can be tuned against proxies: rating criteria, preference signals, acceptance thresholds
- Preferences, shortcuts, and standards can be encoded explicitly in design systems and evaluation rubrics, and implicitly in the data fed to models
- If teams do not consciously develop strategic taste, they risk hard-coding mediocre (or harmful) judgments into systems that operate at scale
- "Implementation bandwidth is getting cheaper, while judgment is getting more valuable"
- "What differentiates good work is no longer the ability to generate ideas, but the ability to discriminate between them"

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Encoding taste is the central challenge of the Founder Simulator. The approaches:

1. **Binary taste signals** — Collect "like / dislike" decisions from the founder on enough examples to train a preference model
2. **Taste rubrics** — Explicitly articulate the criteria behind taste judgments (e.g., "dark themes > light themes," "dense information > whitespace," "mascot integration always")
3. **Comparative pairs** — Present A/B choices to the founder to extract preference vectors
4. **Historical analysis** — Mine git history for approval/rejection patterns on designs, features, and implementations
5. **Taste constitution** — A document of product values and quality standards that the system uses for self-critique

### 3.5 Visionary Founders vs Operators

**The Distinction:**

Research consistently identifies two distinct cognitive modes in leadership:

- **Visionary founders** think decades ahead, see both big pictures and granular details, combine creative and systematic thinking, and are willing to break conventions
- **Operators** excel at execution, optimization, efficiency, and scaling — they make the visionary's ideas real

**Key Finding:** Every legendary visionary had an operational counterweight. Jobs had Tim Cook. Musk has Gwynne Shotwell. Bezos had Andy Jassy. The visionary provides direction and taste; the operator provides reliable execution.

**Cognitive Patterns of Visionary Founders (from Ray Dalio's research):**

Visionaries can "see both big pictures and granular details (and levels in between) and synthesize the perspectives they gain at those different levels, whereas most people just see one or the other." They navigate fluidly between abstraction levels.

Additional patterns:
- **Polymathic knowledge integration** — They draw from multiple unrelated fields
- **Long time horizons** — They think in decades, not quarters
- **Contrarian conviction** — They hold views that most people think are wrong, and they are right
- **Discomfort tolerance** — They can hold unresolved ambiguity without anxiety
- **Simultaneous creation and criticism** — They can generate ideas and immediately evaluate them

**How This Differs from AI:**

Current AI agents are fundamentally operators. They excel at execution (writing code, analyzing data, following instructions) but struggle with:
- Making taste judgments (what should we build?)
- Maintaining vision coherence over time (what does this project want to become?)
- Creative leaps (what would make this 10x better rather than 10% better?)
- Strategic patience (when should we wait rather than act?)

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**

The Visionary Agent must be explicitly trained to operate in visionary mode, not operator mode. This means:
- Generate ideas that the founder has NOT asked for
- Think about where the product should be in 5 years, not just what feature to build next
- Make creative leaps by connecting unrelated domains
- Sometimes say "we should NOT build this" even when it is technically feasible
- Maintain coherence between individual features and the overall product vision

## 4. AI Agent Architecture Review

### 4.1 Chain-of-Thought Prompting

**What It Is:** Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) is a technique where the language model is prompted to generate intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer. Instead of directly outputting a conclusion, the model "shows its work."

**How It Works:**
- The prompt includes examples of step-by-step reasoning
- The model generates a sequence of reasoning steps
- Each step builds on the previous one
- The final answer emerges from the chain

**Strengths:**
- Significantly improves accuracy on reasoning tasks
- Makes the model's reasoning transparent and auditable
- Relatively simple to implement (just add "Let's think step by step")
- Low computational overhead (single forward pass)

**Limitations:**
- Linear and sequential — cannot explore branching possibilities
- Deterministic — one chain, one conclusion
- Cannot backtrack — if an early step is wrong, the whole chain goes wrong
- Not well-suited for creative tasks that require exploring multiple options

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

CoT is the baseline reasoning mode. It is useful for:
- Analyzing a specific product idea (step-by-step evaluation)
- Breaking down a complex decision into sub-decisions
- Explaining the reasoning behind a recommendation

But it is insufficient for the creative, exploratory thinking that product visionaries do. For that, we need Tree-of-Thought.

### 4.2 Tree-of-Thought for Product Ideation

**What It Is:** Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al., NeurIPS 2023) generalizes Chain-of-Thought by generating multiple lines of reasoning in parallel, with the ability to evaluate each branch, backtrack, and explore alternative paths. It frames LLM reasoning as a tree search over partial reasoning paths.

**Key Innovation:**

ToT maintains a tree where:
- Each node is a "thought" — a coherent unit of text representing a partial reasoning step
- The tree branches at each step, exploring multiple possibilities
- A separate evaluation process assesses the promise of each branch using heuristics
- Search algorithms (BFS or DFS) determine which branches to explore further
- The system can look ahead and backtrack, making global choices rather than greedy local ones

**Experimental Results:**

- Game of 24: CoT solved 4% of tasks; ToT solved 74%
- Creative Writing: ToT produced substantially higher-quality outputs
- Mini Crosswords: ToT dramatically outperformed CoT

**Why ToT Matters for Product Ideation:**

Product visionaries do not think linearly. They consider multiple product directions simultaneously, evaluate each one against different criteria, and choose the most promising path — or combine elements from multiple paths. This is exactly what ToT does.

Consider how a founder might think about a new feature:
```
Raw Idea: "Add voice interaction to the elderly companion"
  |
  +-- Path A: Full voice assistant (like Alexa)
  |     Evaluate: Too expensive, too competitive, not differentiated
  |     Backtrack.
  |
  +-- Path B: Voice-to-text with personality adaptation
  |     Evaluate: Moderate cost, leverages existing personality system
  |     Explore further...
  |     +-- Path B1: Real-time voice processing
  |     |     Evaluate: Latency issues for elderly users
  |     +-- Path B2: Async voice messages with text response
  |           Evaluate: Better UX for elderly, lower latency
  |           Choose this path.
  |
  +-- Path C: AI-generated voice responses with adapted speech patterns
        Evaluate: High differentiation, serves the companionship job
        Explore further...
```

This branching, evaluating, and selecting process is exactly how product visionaries reason about product decisions.

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**

ToT should be the **primary reasoning architecture** for the Visionary Agent. Specifically:

1. **Thought Generation** — For each product question, generate 3-5 distinct approaches
2. **Thought Evaluation** — Evaluate each approach against:
   - JTBD alignment (does it serve a real user job?)
   - Effectuation principles (is the loss affordable? does it use available means?)
   - Blue Ocean potential (does it differentiate or just compete?)
   - Founder taste vectors (would the founder be excited by this?)
3. **Search Strategy** — Use BFS for broad exploration, DFS for deep exploration of promising paths
4. **Backtracking** — Explicitly abandon paths that score poorly, with explanations of why
5. **Path Combination** — Allow elements from different branches to be combined into hybrid solutions

### 4.3 Multi-Agent Debate Frameworks

**What It Is:** Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) is a framework where multiple LLM agents independently generate answers, critique each other's reasoning over several rounds, and converge toward a better answer through deliberation.

**How It Works (Standard MAD):**

1. Multiple agents independently generate initial responses in parallel
2. Over several rounds, agents review each other's responses and provide feedback
3. Agents incorporate collective feedback to refine their answers
4. A final aggregation step produces the consensus answer

**Key Research Findings (2025-2026):**

- **Persona-based debate is effective:** Agents prompted to role-play specific personas (skeptic, optimist, domain expert) stimulate more critical thinking and divergent feedback
- **Majority voting accounts for most gains:** Recent research reveals that simple majority voting captures most of the performance improvement attributed to MAD
- **Deliberation maximizes effectiveness:** Requiring agents to explicitly agree/disagree and justify, and to revise only when presented with sound, high-confidence rationales
- **Multi-Agent Reflexion (MAR):** A 2025 framework incorporating diverse reasoning personas and a judge model that synthesizes critiques into unified reflections, separating the processes of acting, diagnosing, critiquing, and aggregating to reduce shared blind spots
- **Adaptive Heterogeneous MAD (A-HMAD):** A 2025 framework that consistently outperforms prior single-model methods and original MAD baselines across six challenging benchmarks

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Multi-Agent Debate is the natural architecture for the Critic/Visionary/Strategist interaction. Instead of a single agent making product decisions, we want:

- **Visionary Agent** (Green/Yellow Hat) — Proposes ideas, sees opportunity, generates excitement
- **Critic Agent** (Black Hat) — Challenges feasibility, identifies risks, enforces quality standards
- **Strategist Agent** (Blue Hat) — Plans implementation, manages scope, sequences deliverables
- **Research Agent** (White Hat) — Provides facts, data, competitive analysis
- **Founder Simulator** (Red Hat) — Provides gut reactions, taste judgments, emotional assessment

These agents debate over 2-3 rounds, with each round producing more refined output. The debate structure ensures that:
- No single perspective dominates
- Risks are identified before they become problems
- Ideas are stress-tested against multiple criteria
- The final output represents a synthesis, not a compromise

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**

```
Round 1: Independent Generation
  Visionary → Product proposal
  Research → Market analysis, user needs
  Strategist → Feasibility and phasing assessment

Round 2: Cross-Critique
  Critic → Reviews all three outputs, identifies gaps and risks
  Founder Simulator → Predicts founder reaction to each element
  All agents revise based on critiques

Round 3: Synthesis
  Strategist → Produces unified plan incorporating all feedback
  Founder Simulator → Final "would the founder approve?" check
  Output → Refined product proposal with implementation plan
```

### 4.4 Constitutional AI for Product Values

**What It Is:** Constitutional AI (CAI), developed by Anthropic, is a technique for aligning AI behavior with a set of principles (a "constitution") using self-supervision. The model critiques and revises its own outputs based on the constitution, then is trained via reinforcement learning using AI-generated feedback.

**How It Works:**

1. **Phase 1 (Self-Critique):** The model generates a response, then critiques it against each principle in the constitution, and revises it to better align with the principles
2. **Phase 2 (RLHF with AI Feedback):** A model is trained via reinforcement learning to prefer outputs that better satisfy the constitution

**Recent Development (January 2026):** Anthropic published a comprehensive new constitution for Claude, shifting from rule-based to reason-based alignment. The constitution explains the logic behind ethical principles rather than prescribing specific behaviors, and establishes a clear priority hierarchy: (1) safety and human oversight, (2) ethical behavior, (3) Anthropic's guidelines, (4) helpfulness.

**Application to Product Values:**

The same mechanism can be adapted for product values instead of safety values. A "Product Constitution" would be a set of principles that the system uses to self-critique product decisions:

**Example Product Constitution for Project Visionary:**

```
PRODUCT CONSTITUTION v1.0
=========================

Priority 1: User Value
  - Every feature must serve a real user job (functional, social, or emotional)
  - The user's experience takes priority over technical elegance
  - If a feature does not make someone's life better, it does not ship

Priority 2: Product Coherence
  - Every feature must strengthen the overall product vision, not dilute it
  - Scope expansion is only justified if it serves the core product thesis
  - Features that create confusion or complexity debt are rejected

Priority 3: Quality Standards
  - Visual quality must meet or exceed the founder's taste thresholds
  - Technical implementation must pass all SDLC gates
  - Content must be researched, cited, and actionable
  - Nothing ships without end-to-end verification

Priority 4: Innovation Ambition
  - Incremental improvement is the floor, not the ceiling
  - The system should always ask "what would make this 10x better?"
  - Creative leaps are valued over safe iterations
  - Blue Ocean thinking over Red Ocean competition

Priority 5: Execution Discipline
  - Every commitment must be deliverable within the stated timeline
  - Scope is managed through phasing, not cutting quality
  - Done means deployed, tested, and verified — not "code complete"
```

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**

The Product Constitution is loaded into every agent as a system-level constraint. After generating any output (product proposal, implementation plan, design decision), the agent:

1. Self-critiques against each principle in priority order
2. Identifies any violations
3. Revises the output to better align
4. If conflicts exist between priorities, higher priorities win

This creates a self-correcting system where product values are enforced automatically, without requiring the founder to catch every issue.

### 4.5 Reflexion and Self-Critique Patterns

**What It Is:** Reflexion (Shinn et al., NeurIPS 2023) is a framework that gives language agents the ability to learn from their mistakes without parameter updates. Instead of updating model weights, the agent generates natural-language reflections about its failures and stores them in an episodic memory buffer for future reference.

**Core Mechanism:**

1. The agent attempts a task
2. If it fails, it generates a verbal reflection: "I failed because..."
3. The reflection is stored in episodic memory
4. On the next attempt, the reflection is included in the agent's context
5. The agent uses the reflection to avoid repeating the same mistake

**Performance:**
- HumanEval coding benchmark: 91% pass@1 (vs 80% for GPT-4 without Reflexion)
- Significant improvements across sequential decision-making, coding, and language reasoning tasks

**2025-2026 Advances:**

- **Multi-Agent Reflexion (MAR):** Incorporates diverse reasoning personas and a judge model that synthesizes critiques, separating the processes of acting, diagnosing, critiquing, and aggregating
- **Reflective Agents as a Core Pattern:** By 2026, agent architectures routinely include self-critique loops, with reflection improving coding benchmarks from 80% to 91%
- **Test-Time Reasoning:** The trend toward "giving models more time to think" through iterative refinement and reflection, rather than building larger models

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

Reflexion provides the **learning mechanism** for the entire agent system. The system should maintain:

1. **Decision Memory:** Records of past product decisions and their outcomes
   - "We chose X, and the founder loved it because Y"
   - "We proposed Z, and the founder rejected it because W"

2. **Anti-Pattern Memory:** A growing library of mistakes to avoid
   - "We declared a feature done without E2E testing — never again"
   - "We generated full scene images instead of compositing overlays — caused visual drift"

3. **Taste Calibration Memory:** A running record of how well the system's taste judgments match the founder's actual reactions
   - "The system predicted the founder would approve this design — the founder did not → calibrate taste vectors"

This maps directly to the existing `.claude/mistakes.log` pattern in the Elderle project, but generalized to a first-class architectural concept.

1. **Context and Business Intent:** Agents have no visibility into the business intent behind the work. They can write code but cannot understand why that code matters.

2. **Specification Ambiguity:** There is a fundamental gap between English specifications and code implementation. The number of ambiguous decision points increases with program length. Models generate features that were not asked for and make shifting assumptions around gaps in requirements.

3. **Architectural Judgment:** AI lacks the experience, judgment, and ownership required to make trade-offs that affect users, stakeholders, or the long-term health of the system.

4. **Reliability:** Even the best agents fail on 19-49% of real-world coding problems. Fully autonomous coding pipelines remain rare (less than 8% of companies).

5. **Scaffolding Matters More Than Models:** Agent scaffolding (the system around the model) matters as much as the underlying model. Three frameworks running identical models scored 17 issues apart on 731 problems.

**Key Insight:** The scaffolding finding is critical for Project Visionary. The value we create is not in the model (which is commodity) but in the scaffolding: the agent roles, the debate framework, the knowledge base, the quality gates, the taste vectors. This is the moat.

### 4.7 Multi-Agent Frameworks: CrewAI, LangGraph, AutoGPT

**CrewAI:**
- Role-based model inspired by real-world organizational structures
- 280% adoption increase in 2025
- Fastest to set up (working multi-agent system in <50 lines of code)

**LangGraph:**
- Graph-based state machine with durable execution
- Reached v1.0 in late 2025; default runtime for LangChain agents
- Best for: Stateful workflows with multiple decision points and parallel processing
- Supports human-in-the-loop (critical for founder review checkpoints)
- Limitation: Steeper learning curve

**AutoGPT:**
- 167,000+ GitHub stars, pioneered fully autonomous AI agents
- Continuously works toward goals by breaking them into subtasks
- Best for: Maximum autonomy, minimal human intervention
- Limitation: Reliability issues, tendency to loop or diverge

**For Project Visionary, LangGraph is the recommended framework:**

1. **State Management:** Product development is inherently stateful. The system needs to track where each idea is in the pipeline, what decisions have been made, and what the current context is.
2. **Human-in-the-Loop:** The founder review checkpoint is non-negotiable. LangGraph has native support for pausing execution and waiting for human input.
3. **Parallel Processing:** Multiple agents (Visionary, Research, Critic) can operate in parallel during debate rounds.
4. **Durable Execution:** Product development spans days/weeks, not minutes. The system needs to persist state across sessions.
5. **Graph Structure:** The agent pipeline (Visionary → Research → Strategist → Critic → Founder Simulator → Builder → Verifier) is naturally a directed graph with cycles and branches.

### 4.8 The Product-Level Thinking Gap

**The Central Problem:**

Current AI agents excel at execution (writing code, generating content, analyzing data) but fail at product-level thinking. The gap manifests in several ways:

1. **No Business Context:** Agents have no visibility into why the code matters, who the users are, or what the competitive landscape looks like. They optimize locally (make this function work) without understanding globally (make this product successful).

2. **No Taste:** Agents cannot distinguish between a technically correct solution and a good solution. They can make something that works but cannot make something that delights.

3. **No Strategic Patience:** Agents default to immediate action. They cannot say "we should wait" or "this is not the right time." Visionary founders know when NOT to build something.

4. **No Scope Judgment:** Agents will build whatever is asked. They cannot say "this feature is out of scope" or "this will create complexity debt that outweighs the benefit." A 2026 study found that models "generate features that weren't asked for, make shifting assumptions around gaps in requirements, and declare success even when tests are failing."

5. **No Vision Coherence:** Agents treat each task independently. They cannot maintain a coherent product vision across months of development, where each decision should strengthen the overall direction.

**What Humans Currently Provide:**

"Humans are the only ones who can recognize when business context changes, when a feature should be cut for scope, or when a security concern outweighs performance gains." Developers delegate easily verifiable, low-stakes tasks to AI while keeping conceptually difficult or design-dependent tasks for themselves.

**The Project Visionary Opportunity:**

Project Visionary aims to close this gap — not by making a better code generator, but by building an AI system that understands WHY things should be built, not just HOW. This requires:
- Encoding product context (who are the users? what is the product vision? what are the quality standards?)
- Building taste judgments (what does "good" look like for this specific product?)
- Implementing scope control (what should NOT be built?)
- Maintaining vision coherence (does this feature strengthen or dilute the overall direction?)
- Enabling strategic patience (when should we wait rather than act?)

## 5. Knowledge Management: Encoding Product Intuition

### 5.1 Nonaka's SECI Model

**Origin:** Ikujiro Nonaka (1990), refined with Hirotaka Takeuchi. The SECI model describes how organizational knowledge is created through the conversion between tacit and explicit knowledge.

**The Four Modes:**

1. **Socialization (Tacit → Tacit):** Knowledge sharing through shared experience. In the founder context: the founder's intuitions are absorbed by close collaborators through working together, watching decisions being made, and absorbing the "feel" of what good looks like. This is how Jony Ive absorbed Jobs' taste by working alongside him for years.

2. **Externalization (Tacit → Explicit):** The critical bottleneck. Converting gut feelings, aesthetic preferences, and decision intuitions into articulated concepts, written documents, and structured frameworks. This is what Phase 1 of Project Visionary aims to do: externalize the founder's tacit knowledge into a Founder Decision Profile.

   Methods for externalization:
   - **Metaphors and analogies** — "The product should feel like a knowledgeable friend, not a medical database"
   - **Models and frameworks** — Decision templates, taste rubrics, quality checklists
   - **Dialogue and confrontation** — Challenging the founder to articulate WHY they prefer one option over another
   - **Narrative reconstruction** — Walking through past decisions and extracting the reasoning

3. **Combination (Explicit → Explicit):** Combining multiple explicit knowledge sources into new, more complex knowledge. In the Project Visionary context: combining the Founder Decision Profile with academic frameworks (this document), competitive analysis, and user research into a comprehensive product strategy.

4. **Internalization (Explicit → Tacit):** Converting explicit knowledge back into tacit understanding. In the AI context: the agent system "internalizing" the Product Constitution and taste vectors so that its outputs naturally align with founder values without explicit checking.

**The Knowledge Spiral:**

The four modes form a spiral: Socialization → Externalization → Combination → Internalization → Socialization (at a higher level). Each cycle expands the organization's knowledge base. In Project Visionary, each product development cycle should expand the system's understanding of what good products look like.

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

The SECI model provides the roadmap for the knowledge encoding pipeline:

```
Phase 1 (Profiling) = Externalization
  Founder's tacit knowledge → Explicit profiles, patterns, taste vectors

Phase 2 (Framework Research) = Combination
  Explicit profiles + Academic frameworks → Comprehensive architecture

Phase 3 (Pattern Extraction) = Combination
  Architecture + Decision templates → Pattern library

Phase 4 (Agent Architecture) = Internalization
  Pattern library → Agent behaviors (constitutional AI, taste vectors)

Phase 5 (Validation) = Socialization
  Agent outputs → Founder reaction → Calibration → Better socialization
```

### 5.2 Organizational Memory Systems

**The Four Layers of AI-Native Organizational Memory (2026):**

1. **Behavioral Memory** — Captures what people actually do (actions, not intentions)
   - In Project Visionary: git commits, PRs, deploy logs, feature usage metrics

2. **Decision Memory** — Records the intent and assumptions behind key choices
   - In Project Visionary: PR/FAQs, architectural decision records (ADRs), design rationale documents

3. **Outcome Memory** — Links actions to measurable consequences
   - In Project Visionary: user metrics after feature launches, founder satisfaction scores, deployment success rates

4. **Learning Memory** — Synthesizes patterns across decisions
   - In Project Visionary: the Reflexion-based episodic memory, the growing case library, the taste calibration record

**Current Industry State:**

AI-powered knowledge management is a $3.1 billion market in 2025, projected to reach $68.7 billion by 2034 (42.9% CAGR). The market growth reflects the industry's recognition that knowledge management is the bottleneck for AI effectiveness.

GenAI has extended AI's role in knowledge creation, with LLMs assisting in synthesizing complex information, generating ideas, drafting content, and facilitating real-time interaction with large-scale knowledge repositories. However, most systems focus on information retrieval, not judgment encoding.

**Implementation in Project Visionary:**

The system needs all four memory layers:

1. **Behavioral Memory:** Automatically capture all actions taken by the agent system (proposals generated, decisions made, code written, tests run)
2. **Decision Memory:** Require every significant decision to have a recorded rationale (why this approach? what alternatives were considered? what principles were applied?)
3. **Outcome Memory:** Track what happened after each decision (founder approved/rejected, users engaged/churned, system stable/broken)
4. **Learning Memory:** Periodically synthesize patterns across the other three layers (what decision patterns lead to founder approval? what patterns lead to user engagement? what patterns predict technical problems?)

### 5.3 Case-Based Reasoning for Product Decisions

**What It Is:** Case-Based Reasoning (CBR) solves new problems by adapting solutions from similar past problems. It is experience-based: the system builds a library of "cases" (past decisions and their outcomes) and retrieves the most relevant ones when facing a new decision.

**The CBR Cycle:**

1. **Retrieve** — Given a new problem, search the case library for similar past cases
2. **Reuse** — Adapt the past solution to fit the new problem
3. **Revise** — Evaluate whether the adapted solution works in the new context
4. **Retain** — If the solution works, store it as a new case for future reference

**Application to Product Decisions:**

Each past product decision becomes a case:
```
Case: QCR Gamification Decision
================================
Context: QCR was a utility tool for car information
Trigger: Research on Octalysis gamification framework
Decision: Add gamification as core retention architecture
Rationale: JTBD analysis showed users want to feel smart, not just informed
            Gamification serves social/emotional jobs beyond functional
Outcome: Gamification became the retention architecture, not just a feature
Principles Applied: Lateral Thinking (creative leap), JTBD (emotional jobs),
                     Effectuation (Lemonade — surprise became core feature)
```

When the system faces a new decision (e.g., "Should we add social features to Elderle?"), it retrieves similar cases:
- Cases where social/community features were added to utility products
- Cases where the founder evaluated features that served social jobs
- Cases where scope expansion strengthened vs diluted the product

The retrieved cases inform the recommendation, and the new decision (with its outcome) is retained as a new case.

**Relevance to Project Visionary:**

CBR provides the "experience" layer that current AI agents lack. Instead of reasoning from first principles every time, the system can draw on past decisions as precedents. This is how experienced founders operate: they have seen enough decisions to have pattern recognition about what works and what does not.

### 5.4 Encoding "Taste" in AI Systems

**The State of the Art:**

A January 2026 paper (arXiv:2601.17134) — "Deconstructing Taste: Toward a Human-Centered AI Framework for Modeling Consumer Aesthetic Perceptions" — proposes an integrated human-centered computational framework that links subjective evaluations with domain-specific features and computer vision-based measures. This is the most relevant academic work for Project Visionary's taste encoding challenge.

**Key Findings:**

1. **Taste can be encoded as data, modeled as a function, and optimized as a reward.** This opens the door to LLMs trained not just to be helpful, but to have taste.

2. **Proxies work, but must be carefully designed.** Models can be tuned against rating criteria, preference signals, and acceptance thresholds. But if teams do not consciously design these proxies, they risk encoding mediocrity.

3. **Interpretability is critical.** Models trained on image-level preferences can reproduce stylistic tendencies without exposing the principles designers need to critique, adapt, or subvert them. For Project Visionary, the taste model must be interpretable so the founder can audit and calibrate it.

4. **Training data shapes taste.** When algorithms train on dominant datasets, they replicate dominant aesthetics. For Project Visionary, the training data must come from the specific founder's decisions, not industry averages.

**Proposed Taste Encoding Architecture for Project Visionary:**

```
Layer 1: Taste Rubric (Explicit Rules)
  - Dark themes preferred over light themes
  - Dense information layouts preferred over whitespace-heavy layouts
  - Mascot integration enhances brand identity
  - Research depth > surface-level content
  - Animation used sparingly, with purpose

Layer 2: Taste Vectors (Learned Preferences)
  - Vector embeddings of approved vs rejected designs
  - Similarity scoring against approved examples
  - Continuously calibrated against new founder decisions

Layer 3: Taste Constitution (Product Values)
  - Quality thresholds for each content type
  - Rejection criteria (what triggers "this isn't good enough"?)
  - Aspiration criteria (what triggers "this is exactly right"?)

Layer 4: Taste Memory (Historical Patterns)
  - Every approval/rejection with rationale
  - Trend analysis (has the founder's taste evolved over time?)
  - Context-dependent taste (different standards for different product areas)
```

## 6. Academic Foundation

### 6.1 AI Product Management

**Key Paper: "Where does AI play a major role in the new product development and product management process?"** (Management Review Quarterly, May 2025)

This structured literature review analyzed 190 publications and five expert interviews, mapping AI applications across the product development lifecycle. Key findings:
- AI methods like sentiment analysis, knowledge extraction, and demand forecasting dominate in early-phase activities
- The gap: AI excels at analyzing existing data but struggles with the creative, judgment-intensive decisions that define product direction
- Most AI in product management is still decision-support, not decision-making

**Key Paper: "Agentic AI in Product Management: A Co-Evolutionary Model"** (arXiv, 2025)

This paper proposes that product management is evolving from managing feature lists to designing, curating, and aligning agentic AI systems. Agent-based systems can autonomously run A/B tests, iterate on features, or modify workflows based on real-time telemetry.

**Implication for Project Visionary:** The academic literature confirms that AI in product management is currently limited to analysis and optimization. No existing system attempts what Project Visionary proposes: encoding product vision and founder cognition into an agent system. This is a genuine research frontier.

### 6.2 Automated Software Engineering Decision-Making

**Key Paper: "The Rise of AI Teammates in Software Engineering (SE) 3.0"** (arXiv, July 2025)

Introduces AIDev, the first large-scale dataset capturing how AI coding agents operate in the wild: 456,000+ pull requests by five leading agents across 61,000 repositories and 47,000 developers. Key findings:
- SE 3.0 envisions software development as an intent-driven, conversational process
- Agents operate at the task level: reading codebases, planning changes, executing tools, refactoring, running tests, submitting PRs
- But they do NOT operate at the product level: understanding user needs, making scope decisions, maintaining vision coherence

**Key Paper: "How AI Coding Agents Modify Code: A Large-Scale Study of GitHub Pull Requests"** (arXiv, January 2026)

Analyzed how AI agents modify code in practice. Finding: agents make primarily local changes (single-file edits, bug fixes, refactors) but struggle with global changes (architectural decisions, cross-system redesigns, product-level modifications).

**Key Paper: "Autonomous Agents in Software Development: A Vision Paper"** (Springer, 2025)

Demonstrates success in automating the entire SDLC based on software specifications. But the key assumption: the specification must already exist. The paper does not address who creates the specification or how product decisions are made.

**Implication for Project Visionary:** The SE research confirms that the gap between "writes code" and "makes good product decisions" is real and unsolved. Existing agents need specifications handed to them. Project Visionary aims to generate those specifications by encoding product vision.

### 6.3 Creative AI Agent Systems

**Key Paper: "The paradox of creativity in generative AI: high performance, human-like bias, and limited differential evaluation"** (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025)

AI models demonstrate significant potential for generating creative outputs, but they lack consciousness, intentionality, and genuine metacognitive capacities. Their outputs reflect probabilistic inferences over learned patterns. The paradox: AI can produce creative-seeming output but cannot evaluate its own creativity.

**Key Paper: "Human agents, generative AI, and innovation: A formal model of hybrid creative process"** (ScienceDirect, 2025)

Proposes a formal model for human-AI collaboration in creative work. Key finding: the optimal arrangement is for AI to generate diverse options rapidly while humans provide evaluation, selection, and refinement. This matches Project Visionary's architecture: agents generate, the Founder Simulator evaluates, the human founder makes final selections.

**Key Paper: "Artificial Intelligence and Creativity"** (Philosophy Compass, 2025)

A philosophical analysis of whether AI can be truly creative. Conclusion: AI can be creative in the "combinatorial" sense (novel combinations of existing elements) but not in the "transformational" sense (fundamentally changing the conceptual space). Transformational creativity requires understanding why rules exist, not just following them.

**Implication for Project Visionary:** The creativity research suggests that Project Visionary should not aim for fully autonomous creative product thinking. Instead, it should aim for a hybrid system where AI handles combinatorial creativity (generating many novel combinations) while the human founder provides transformational creativity (changing the rules when needed) and evaluative judgment (selecting the best option).

### 6.4 Knowledge Transfer in AI Development

**Key Paper: "Unveiling the Unspoken: A Conceptual Framework for AI-Enabled Tacit Knowledge Co-Evolution"** (MDPI, 2025)

Proposes a conceptual framework for how AI and human tacit knowledge co-evolve. Key insight: the relationship between AI and tacit knowledge is not one-way (human → AI extraction). It is co-evolutionary: AI systems that capture tacit knowledge also change how humans develop and express tacit knowledge.

**Key Paper: "Generative AI Meets Knowledge Management: Insights From Software Development Practices"** (Wiley, 2025)

Examines how GenAI changes knowledge management in software development contexts. Key finding: developers using GenAI tools develop different knowledge patterns than those who do not. The tool becomes part of the knowledge ecosystem, not just a knowledge store.

**Key Paper: "Transforming organizational knowledge creation through artificial intelligence"** (Emerald, 2025)

A systematic review of the emergent literature on how AI transforms organizational knowledge creation. Finding: AI can amplify all four modes of the SECI model, but the externalization mode (tacit → explicit) remains the most challenging and the most valuable.

**Implication for Project Visionary:** The knowledge transfer research validates that AI-enabled tacit knowledge externalization is both the hardest and the most important challenge. Phase 1's profiling work (git archaeology, session log analysis) is the externalization step. The quality of everything downstream depends on how well we extract the founder's tacit knowledge.

### 6.5 Founder Cognition Patterns

**Key Paper: "Entrepreneurial passion, alertness and opportunity recognition"** (Sage, 2025)

Three types of passion (inventing, founding, developing) all enhance opportunity recognition through entrepreneurial alertness. Passion for inventing and founding boost alertness most in highly dynamic environments.

**Key Paper: "Predicting entrepreneurship from brain structure and neural responses to risk"** (PMC, 2025)

Brain responses to risk (measured via fMRI) are the strongest predictor of entrepreneurial status. Entrepreneurs process risk at a neurological level differently from non-entrepreneurs. This has implications for the Founder Simulator: it should model risk-processing patterns, not just risk-tolerance scores.

**Key Paper: "Deeper knowledge of entrepreneurs in decision-making: Innovating through neuroentrepreneurship"** (ScienceDirect, 2025)

Significant differences in emotional and cognitive responses between novice and experienced entrepreneurs. Experienced founders worry about different things than novices and process decisions differently. The Founder Simulator should be calibrated to the founder's experience level.

**Key Paper: "The rhythm of effective entrepreneurs' decision-making process"** (Springer, 2025)

Entrepreneurs use a rhythmic pattern of alertness scanning, search, and cognitive style switching. The effective founder does not use a single cognitive style; they switch between styles based on the type of decision.

**Implication for Project Visionary:** The founder cognition research shows that effective founders are not consistently intuitive or consistently analytical. They switch styles rhythmically based on context. The Founder Simulator needs to model this switching behavior, not just an average style.

## 7. Synthesis: The Combined Framework

### 7.1 The Visionary Agent Architecture

Synthesizing all research, the Visionary Agent should operate as follows:

**Input:** A raw idea, expressed as a brief natural language description

**Processing Pipeline:**

```
Step 1: MEANS ASSESSMENT (Effectuation — Bird-in-Hand)
  - What existing capabilities can we leverage?
  - What does the team know?
  - What resources are already available?
  - What adjacent problems have we already solved?

Step 2: JOB ANALYSIS (JTBD)
  - What functional job would this serve?
  - What social job would this serve?
  - What emotional job would this serve?
  - What are users currently "hiring" for this job?
  - Where are the gaps in current solutions?

Step 3: FIRST PRINCIPLES DECOMPOSITION
  - What are the fundamental truths about this problem?
  - Which "rules" of the current category are conventions, not constraints?
  - What would a solution look like if built from scratch?

Step 4: TREE-OF-THOUGHT EXPLORATION
  - Generate 3-5 distinct product directions
  - Evaluate each against:
    * JTBD alignment score
    * Effectuation principles (affordable loss, crazy quilt, lemonade)
    * Blue Ocean potential (strategy canvas analysis)
    * Taste vector alignment
    * Technical feasibility
  - Prune unlikely paths, explore promising ones deeper
  - Allow path combination for hybrid solutions

Step 5: LATERAL THINKING ENHANCEMENT
  - Apply random entry technique (connect to unrelated domain)
  - Apply provocation ("What if we did the opposite?")
  - Apply concept extraction (what is the underlying principle?)
  - Apply cross-domain analogy

Step 6: PR/FAQ GENERATION (Working Backwards)
  - Write the press release for the best product direction
  - Write the FAQ addressing hard questions
  - Evaluate: would a customer be excited by this press release?

Step 7: CONSTITUTIONAL SELF-CRITIQUE
  - Check against Product Constitution (all 5 priorities)
  - Identify violations
  - Revise to better align
  - Document any priority conflicts and how they were resolved
```

**Output:** A Product Proposal containing:
- PR/FAQ document
- JTBD analysis (functional/social/emotional)
- Strategy Canvas (competitive positioning)
- Means assessment (what we already have that helps)
- Risk assessment (affordable loss analysis)
- Tree-of-Thought reasoning trace (for transparency)
- Constitutional compliance report

```
    - Similarity metrics for retrieval
    - Index by decision type, product area, and principle

Phase 4: CONSTITUTIONAL CODIFICATION (CAI)
  Input: Founder Decision Profile + Case Library + Quality Standards
  Process: Extract constitutional principles from patterns
  Output: Product Constitution
    - Priority-ordered principles
    - Quality thresholds for each product area
    - Rejection criteria and aspiration criteria
    - Self-critique prompts for each principle

Phase 5: TASTE VECTOR TRAINING
  Input: Approved/rejected designs, features, implementations
  Process: Contrastive learning on preference pairs
  Output: Taste Vectors
    - Visual taste (design preferences)
    - Technical taste (architecture preferences)
    - Content taste (depth, style, format preferences)
    - Scope taste (when to expand vs focus)
    - Innovation taste (when to be conservative vs bold)
```

### 7.3 The Decision Loop

Every product decision in the system follows this loop:

```
1. SITUATION ASSESSMENT
   - What is the current context?
   - What triggered this decision?
   - What type of decision is this? (strategic, tactical, aesthetic, technical)

2. CASE RETRIEVAL (CBR)
   - Find similar past decisions
   - Note their outcomes and rationale
   - Identify relevant differences from the current situation

3. FRAMEWORK APPLICATION
   - Apply the most relevant cognitive framework:
     * Strategic positioning → Blue Ocean
     * User needs → JTBD
     * Creative ideation → Lateral Thinking + ToT
     * Uncertainty → Effectuation
     * Process → Design Thinking
     * Communication → Working Backwards

4. MULTI-AGENT DEBATE
   - Visionary proposes
   - Critic challenges
   - Researcher provides data
   - Strategist plans
   - Founder Simulator predicts reaction
   - 2-3 rounds of refinement

5. CONSTITUTIONAL CHECK
   - Self-critique against Product Constitution
   - Priority resolution for any conflicts
   - Taste vector alignment check

6. FOUNDER REVIEW (when required)
   - Present proposal with reasoning trace
   - Capture approval/rejection with rationale
   - Calibrate system based on outcome

7. LEARNING UPDATE
   - Store decision as new case in CBR library
   - Update Reflexion memory if relevant
   - Calibrate taste vectors if taste judgment was involved
   - Update any anti-patterns identified
```

### 7.4 The Quality Gate System

Drawing from Stripe/Notion/Linear product cultures and the Elderle SDLC system:

```
GATE 1: VISION CHECK
  - Does this serve a real user job? (JTBD)
  - Is this differentiated? (Blue Ocean)
  - Does the founder care about this? (Taste vectors)
  - Pass criteria: All three affirmative

GATE 2: DESIGN REVIEW
  - Is the design quality above taste threshold? (Taste vectors)
  - Is the UX coherent with existing product? (Product Constitution)
  - Has the founder reviewed mockups? (Human checkpoint)
  - Pass criteria: All three affirmative, founder approval

GATE 3: IMPLEMENTATION REVIEW
  - Does the code pass all tests? (Automated)
  - Does the architecture serve long-term goals? (10-year thinking)
  - Is the scope appropriate? (Scope taste vector)
  - Pass criteria: Tests pass, architecture review, scope check

GATE 4: DEPLOYMENT VERIFICATION
  - Is it deployed to the correct environment? (Automated check)
  - Does the actual output match expectations? (Playwright visual check)
  - Does it work end-to-end, not just in isolation? (E2E test)
  - Pass criteria: All three verified with evidence

GATE 5: OUTCOME EVALUATION
  - Did users engage with this? (Metrics)
  - Did it strengthen or dilute the product? (Product coherence)
  - What did we learn? (Reflexion update)
  - Pass criteria: Learning captured regardless of outcome
```

## 8. Gaps: What We Need to Invent

### 8.1 The Taste Encoding Problem

**The Gap:** No existing framework can fully capture and reproduce a specific individual's aesthetic and product taste. Current approaches (taste rubrics, preference vectors, constitutional principles) capture aspects of taste but miss the holistic, contextual nature of human aesthetic judgment.

**Why It Matters:** Taste is the bottleneck. If the system cannot distinguish between a technically correct product and a genuinely good product, it will produce mediocrity at scale. The 2026 article "Taste is the New Bottleneck" argues that as AI agents get better at execution, taste becomes the most valuable — and the most difficult to automate — skill.

**What Exists:**
- Preference learning from binary signals (approved/rejected)
- Taste rubrics (explicit rules)
- Constitutional self-critique (principle-based evaluation)
- Aesthetic quality assessment via deep learning (image-level)

**What We Need to Invent:**
- A taste encoding that captures the CONTEXTUAL nature of taste (the same element may be good in one context and bad in another)
- A taste model that evolves as the founder's taste evolves (taste is not static)
- A taste evaluation that works across modalities (visual design, code architecture, content quality, feature scope) with a unified framework
- A way to distinguish between taste preferences (subjective, could go either way) and quality standards (objective, must meet threshold)

**Proposed Research Direction:**
- Build a "taste space" with multiple dimensions (visual, technical, content, scope, innovation)
- Train on preference pairs from historical founder decisions
- Use contrastive learning to separate "better" from "worse" within each dimension
- Implement a calibration loop where the system's taste predictions are compared against actual founder reactions and adjusted

### 8.2 The Scope Judgment Problem

**The Gap:** No existing AI system can make good scope judgments — knowing when to expand a feature, when to cut it, when to defer it, and when to kill it entirely.

**Why It Matters:** Scope management is one of the most important product skills. Every idea has potential; the art is knowing which ideas to pursue NOW versus LATER versus NEVER. Current AI agents will build whatever is asked, without questioning whether it should be built.

**What Exists:**
- Affordable loss principle (Effectuation) — invest up to what you can lose
- JTBD analysis — prioritize by job importance
- Blue Ocean strategy canvas — prioritize by differentiation potential

**What We Need to Invent:**
- A scope evaluation model that considers: current product phase, team capacity, technical debt, product coherence, user needs, and competitive position
- A "scope taste" that learns from the founder's historical scope decisions (when did they expand? when did they cut? what criteria drove the decision?)
- A mechanism for proactively suggesting scope reductions, not just accepting everything

**Proposed Research Direction:**
- Build a case library of scope decisions (expand/cut/defer/kill) with context and outcome
- Train a scope judgment model on these cases
- Implement a "scope advisor" agent that reviews every feature proposal and recommends: Build Now / Defer / Cut / Kill, with reasoning

### 8.3 The Creative Leap Problem

**The Gap:** AI can generate novel combinations of existing elements (combinatorial creativity) but cannot make transformational creative leaps — the kind that redefine a product category.

**Why It Matters:** The most valuable product decisions are creative leaps: QCR's transformation from tools to gamified platform, Elderle's addition of therapeutic stage progression. These are not incremental improvements; they are category redefinitions. No existing AI architecture can reliably produce such leaps.

**What Exists:**
- Lateral Thinking techniques (random entry, provocation, concept extraction)
- Tree-of-Thought exploration (branching and evaluating)
- Cross-domain analogy (finding parallels in other fields)

**What We Need to Invent:**
- A mechanism for injecting genuinely unexpected connections (not just within the training distribution)
- A way to evaluate creative leaps before implementation (how do you know a wild idea is brilliant rather than crazy?)
- A calibration of the system's "creative ambition level" — too conservative produces incremental work; too aggressive produces impractical ideas

**Proposed Research Direction:**
- Build a "creative stimulus library" of concepts from diverse, unrelated fields
- Implement forced connection exercises: "What would [concept from field X] look like in [our product]?"
- Train on the founder's historical creative leaps to learn the pattern: what triggered the leap, how was it evaluated, and what made it succeed?
- Implement a "creative ambition dial" that the founder can adjust based on the current need

### 8.4 The Founder Simulation Problem

**The Gap:** No existing system can reliably simulate a specific individual's decision patterns with enough fidelity to serve as a useful review checkpoint.

**Why It Matters:** The Founder Simulator is the quality gate between autonomous agent work and human review. If it can reliably predict when the founder would approve vs reject, it reduces the number of human review cycles needed. If it cannot, it is just another opinion that the founder must evaluate.

**What Exists:**
- Big Five personality modeling
- System 1/2 decision pattern analysis
- Taste vector matching
- Constitutional self-critique

**What We Need to Invent:**
- A simulation model that captures the founder's decision RHYTHM (when they are generous vs strict, based on context)
- A confidence calibration: the Founder Simulator should know when it is confident in its prediction and when it is uncertain, and escalate to human review in uncertain cases
- A mechanism for the simulation to improve over time as it receives more data on actual founder reactions
- A way to model the founder's EVOLUTION — their taste, priorities, and decision patterns change over time

**Proposed Research Direction:**
- Start with a simple model: match against historical decisions using case-based reasoning
- Add confidence scoring: for each prediction, estimate probability of correctness
- Implement a calibration loop: track predictions vs actual outcomes, adjust model
- Build in temporal weighting: recent decisions are more indicative of current preferences than old decisions
- Implement an "uncertainty threshold": below a certain confidence, automatically escalate to human review

### 8.5 The Long-Horizon Coherence Problem

**The Gap:** No existing AI agent architecture can maintain product vision coherence across months of development. Agents treat each task independently, leading to drift, contradiction, and loss of direction over time.

**Why It Matters:** A product is not a collection of features. It is a coherent system where every element reinforces the whole. A visionary founder maintains this coherence intuitively — they can feel when a feature is "on brand" or "off brand," even if they cannot immediately articulate why. Current agents cannot do this.

**What Exists:**
- Product Constitution (values that each decision is checked against)
- Case library (historical precedent for decisions)
- Reflexion memory (learning from past mistakes)

**What We Need to Invent:**
- A "product coherence model" that tracks the overall direction of the product and flags when a proposed change contradicts or dilutes it
- A "vision document" that evolves over time and serves as the source of truth for what the product is becoming
- A mechanism for detecting when the product is drifting from its original vision vs legitimately evolving into something better
- A way to distinguish between coherent evolution (the product is growing in a consistent direction) and incoherent drift (the product is becoming a collection of unrelated features)

**Proposed Research Direction:**
- Maintain a living "Product Vision Document" that is updated after every significant decision
- Implement a "coherence score" that measures how well each proposed feature aligns with the current vision
- Build a "vision evolution tracker" that distinguishes between legitimate pivots (supported by evidence) and drift (unintentional divergence)
- Implement a "vision review" at regular intervals (every N decisions) where the Visionary Agent explicitly evaluates whether the product is still on track

### 8.6 Research Roadmap for Closing Gaps

| Gap | Priority | Approach | Estimated Effort | Dependencies |
|-----|----------|----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Taste Encoding | P0 — Must solve | Contrastive learning on founder decisions | 2-3 weeks | Phase 1 profiling complete |
| Founder Simulation | P0 — Must solve | CBR + Big Five + confidence calibration | 3-4 weeks | Phase 1 profiling complete |
| Scope Judgment | P1 — Important | Case library + scope advisor agent | 2 weeks | Case library built |
| Long-Horizon Coherence | P1 — Important | Product vision document + coherence scoring | 2 weeks | Product Constitution defined |
| Creative Leap | P2 — Nice to have | Stimulus library + forced connections | 1-2 weeks | Visionary Agent operational |

**Critical Dependency:** Almost everything depends on Phase 1 (Profiling). The quality of the founder's externalized knowledge determines the ceiling for all downstream capabilities. Invest heavily in Phase 1.
